Executive Summary - Main Message

The Guild's $2.02M receivables position is fundamentally sound - 53% is not yet due, and payment follow-up improved through the quarter.

The headline 61.5% collection rate understates health because $1.07M of the $2.02M owed simply hasn't reached its due date. Only 17.9% of invoices are genuinely overdue. The real risks are narrower and specific: sponsorship drives 80% of outstanding receivables (revenue-type concentration) and 128 members (34%) exceed their credit limit - but the main cause is limits undersized for sponsorship invoices, not poor payers. On-time collection improved from 40% to 64% through the quarter.

On-time collection: 40% -> 64% Sponsorship = 80% of outstanding receivables 128 members over credit limit 80% of book clears by mid-April

03
Collections - Maturity Check

March's 26.5% collection rate is a timing effect, not a collection collapse.

Collection rate by issue month measures what share of each month's billing has been paid by the snapshot - it is affected by how much time each invoice has had to be paid.

Two metrics, two stories: Issue-month collection rate and on-time collection rate are different measures. Issue-month rate is affected by how long invoices have had before payment is due (newer invoices often have not reached their due date yet, which lowers the rate). On-time collection measures punctuality among invoices that have already been paid - it is not affected by maturity. Sections 03 and 04 separate these clearly.
Collection Rate by Issue Month
How much of each month's invoicing has been collected by 31 Mar 2026
Highlighted Insights

March invoices couldn't have been collected yet. They were still within their 14-30 day terms at the snapshot - many weren't due until April. The 26.5% is timing, not failure.

January's 84.7% is the mature reference point. With a full quarter to be paid, January's cohort shows what collection looks like once invoices have had time to settle.

Do not act on March 26.5% as a problem. Re-measure the March cohort at end-April once those invoices pass their terms. If it remains below 70%, then investigate.

On-time collection improved from 40% to 64% - payment follow-up strengthened.

Unlike the issue-month rate, on-time collection measures punctuality of actually paid invoices - unaffected by invoice maturity. This is the reliable performance signal.

Diagnosis - Cause Analysis

Over-limit exposure is a credit-limit design gap, not poor payment behaviour.

128 of 382 members (34%) exceed their credit limit, tying up $1.23M - 61% of all outstanding receivables. Three steps trace the main cause.

STEP 1 - CONFIRM

Is the problem real?

"How many members are over their credit limit?"

128 of 382 members with open balances (34%) exceed their assigned credit limit. Their combined outstanding receivables is $1.23M - 61% of all receivables. Peak utilisation reaches 926%. Overdue is spread proportionally across all tiers - not localised to one segment.

-> Yes - a third of members with balances are over their limit, holding the majority of outstanding receivables.
STEP 2 - ISOLATE

Where does it concentrate?

"Which tiers are worst affected?"

Associate and Standard tiers are hardest hit. A single $7,725 sponsorship invoice exceeds an Associate limit ($2,000, x3.9) and a Standard limit ($5,000, x1.5). Premium and Corporate limits absorb it - Corporate has zero over-limit members.

-> The problem hits tiers where limits are too small for sponsorship invoices.
STEP 3 - EXPLAIN

Why is it happening?

"Are these poor payers, or bad limits?"

This is a mismatch between invoice size and credit limits. Two unpaid sponsorship invoices push any Standard or Associate member over their limit regardless of payment behaviour. The limits weren't sized for sponsorship-scale billing.

-> The fix is credit-limit policy, not collections.
Max Invoice ($7,725) as % of Credit Limit by Tier
Anything above 100% means a single sponsorship invoice can breach the limit
✓ Main Cause - Supported by Evidence

The 34% over-limit rate is a limit-setting effect. Credit limits were set by tier without accounting for sponsorship-scale invoices ($4,235-$7,725). Re-sizing limits to match typical invoice values would eliminate most of the apparent credit-risk exposure without changing a single collection process.

Conclusion

Five things to take away.

01

Cash position is sound

53% of the $2.02M is not yet due. Only 17.9% of invoices are overdue, and 64% of overdue outstanding amount is under 30 days - fresh and recoverable.

02

Payment follow-up is improving

On-time collection rose from 40% to 64%. The March collection-rate drop (26.5%) is caused by invoice timing, not a real decline.

03

Sponsorship = 80% of outstanding receivables

Concentration is by revenue type, not by customer (top customer with money outstanding 1.7%). Under stress, a 14-day sponsorship delay traps $304K of cash.

Data Notes and Limitations

Single-quarter analysis only. Three monthly data points are a snapshot, not a trajectory. Cannot establish long-term trends or seasonality.
Synthetic dataset modelled on a realistic Xero/BAS-style export. The process and method are identical to production; numbers are illustrative.
Forecast is based on payment timing, not machine learning - appropriate for single-quarter data. 52% uses member history; 48% uses tier-average fallback.
Payment timing is partly modelled. Lags were reconstructed during cleaning for corrupted rows. Collection-speed metrics are indicative, not fully observed.
Unknown records exist: 181 Unknown-industry customers, 75 Unknown-category invoices (imputed during cleaning). Real shares are understated.
Credit limits are tier-based proxies ($2K-$20K), not formally credit-assessed. "Over limit" signals relative exposure, not a credit breach.
No 90+ day overdue partly reflects the 90-day analysis window - no invoice can structurally exceed ~90 days overdue. Aging severity is a lower bound.
GST sourced from invoice data, not derived. GST-free items carry GST = 0. Outstanding GST = sum of the actual GST column on unpaid invoices.
